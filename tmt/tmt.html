"He'd lost someone he'd built a life with right in the middle of that life. Suddenly, I realized that there was something worse than running, worse even than the schools. There was loss" (Dimaline 99).

The Marrow Thieves is a dystopian book that explores moral dilemmas in a tough world. The characters have to make difficult choices that test their moral values, challenge their identity and culture as they navigate through a harsh reality. Near the start of the book, French talks about his nickname Frenchie “I was nicknamed Frenchie as much for my name as for my people—the Metis. I came from a long line of hunters, trappers, and voyageurs. But now, with most of the rivers cut into pieces and lakes left as grey sludge puckers on the landscape, my own history seemed like a myth along the lines of dragons” (Dimaline 24). French tells us how his culture are perceived primarily through the lens of their ethnic background. The destruction of the natural landscape and the disappearance of traditional practices have eroded their connection to their history. I relate to this theme of grappling with identity. It made me reflect on times when I felt conflicted about my identity and tried to conform to fit societal expectations. In the middle of the novel, French says, "He'd lost someone he'd built a life with right in the middle of that life. Suddenly, I realized that there was something worse than running, worse even than the schools. There was loss" (Dimaline 99). This resonates deeply when reflecting on personal experiences of loss, Losing my grandmother was like losing a vital part of my life, leaving a big hole in my heart. We had this amazing connection built over the years, and her absence went beyond just not being there physically. It was the love, guidance, and special memories we shared that I missed the most. Her passing made me realize that there's something even worse than the pain of losing someone—it's the lasting emptiness that comes with it. Just like the characters in "The Marrow Thieves" who face tough situations, my own experience taught me that the emotional toll of grief is way heavier than any external hardships. It reaches deep into your soul, leaving a mark that goes beyond the immediate sadness. Dealing with loss becomes a journey of healing and understanding the complicated nature of grief. It also helps me relate better to others who've gone through similar losses and appreciate the inner strength it takes to navigate through the aftermath of such a profound emotional upheaval.
